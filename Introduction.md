## 1. Introduction

The standard graphical treatment of a quadratic equation `ax² + bx + c = 0`
proceeds by plotting the parabola `y = ax² + bx + c` and locating the points
where it crosses the horizontal axis. The axis is fixed. The curve changes with
every equation. Roots are where the varying curve meets the invariant zero line.

This works. It is the method found in every textbook, taught in every classroom,
and there is nothing wrong with it. But it encodes a choice — a choice about
which object to hold still — that is rarely made explicit, and never questioned.

This paper questions it.

---

Consider what the standard method is actually doing. It fixes a horizontal blade
— the x-axis — and pushes a different curve into it for every new equation. The
blade stays. The meat moves. Roots are where the meat hits the blade.

Now consider holding the meat still instead.

Fix the parabola `y = x²` permanently in the plane. For any quadratic equation,
move everything except `x²` to the right side. What remains on the right is
always a straight line — a slope and an intercept, nothing more. The roots of
the equation are exactly where that line cuts the fixed parabola. The curve never
moves. Only the line changes.

We call this **Diagonal Cutting**.

---

The name reflects the geometry. In the standard method the cutting motion is
vertical — the curve descends to meet the axis. In Diagonal Cutting the blade
is a line moving at an angle through the fixed curve. The cut is diagonal. The
parabola is the board. The line is the knife.

Same roots. Different grip.

---

What changes when you hold it this way is not the algebra — the roots are still
found by the same procedures — but what is *visible*. Vieta's formulas appear
as properties of the line's slope and intercept. The discriminant becomes a
statement about whether the line cuts, touches, or misses the parabola — readable
from the picture before any calculation is performed. Complex roots acquire a
geometric expression as the gap between the line and the curve when they fail to
meet. And the pattern, followed honestly across degrees, leads to an elementary
geometric statement of Bézout's theorem.

The algebraic content throughout is classical. The contribution is the
reorientation, and the observations that follow from holding the wrong thing
still — or rather, from deciding that the parabola was the right thing to fix
all along.

---
